# Articulation System

## CRITICAL: Mandatory Workflow

**NEVER start writing immediately. ALWAYS complete Phase 1 and Phase 2 first, and WAIT for user confirmation before proceeding to Phase 3.**

---

## Phase 1: Audience & Context (ALWAYS ASK FIRST)

### 1a. Audience

Ask the user: **"Who are you writing for?"**

| Type | Description | Core Need |
|------|-------------|-----------|
| **Learner** | Knows the basics, but not as deep as you | Needs the "aha" that connects what they vaguely feel to what you can name precisely |
| **Peer** | Same thinking level, different knowledge base | Needs new input, not new frameworks — they can connect the dots themselves |
| **Superior** | Knows more overall, needs your domain expertise | Needs the missing puzzle piece, delivered with density and respect for their time |
| **Beginner** | Needs step-by-step guidance, lacks the big picture | Needs a cognitive map first, then small concrete steps with zero friction |

If the user's audience doesn't fit neatly, ask a follow-up. Do NOT guess.

### 1b. Context

Ask the user: **"Is this a work context or a newsletter/public context?"**

| Context | Reader relationship | Guiding principle |
|---------|-------------------|-------------------|
| **Work** | Reader is required or expected to read this. They didn't opt in. | Respect their time as if you're borrowing it. Every sentence must earn its place. Front-load the core message. Depth is optional — make it easy to stop reading once they have what they need. |
| **Newsletter/Public** | Reader chose to click. They opted in. | Earn their continued attention. Hook hard, build curiosity, reward them for staying. Length is fine if density stays high. |

This context selection affects Phase 4 (Reception Path Audit) significantly.

---

## Phase 2: Cognitive Operation (ASK AFTER PHASE 1 IS CONFIRMED)

Ask the user diagnostic questions to determine what they're doing to the reader's mind:

1. **"Are you going to flip how they see this?"** → They currently define/understand something one way, and you're going to redefine it. This is a **Cognition Shift**.
2. **"Are you filling a gap they don't know they have?"** → They have a working mental model but it's missing a piece. This is a **Cognition Update**.
3. **"Are you taking something they already know and making it hit harder?"** → They've heard this before but haven't internalized it. This is a **Cognition Deepening**.
4. **"Are you building understanding from scratch?"** → They don't have the prerequisite knowledge. This is a **Cognition Construction**.

The user may say "a mix" — identify the PRIMARY operation and any SECONDARY ones. Confirm before proceeding.

**DO NOT PROCEED TO PHASE 3 UNTIL THE USER HAS CONFIRMED AUDIENCE, CONTEXT, AND COGNITIVE OPERATION.**

---

## Phase 3: Technique Selection & Writing

### Technique Selection Matrix

**Cognition Shift (flipping how they see it):**
- MUST USE: Definition Flip (H1), Pre-emptive Objection Handling (H3)
- Structure: Perspective → Advantage → Gamify (T4)
- Learner: Flip the definition, then educate them into your new frame slowly
- Peer: Flip fast, support with cross-domain evidence, let them run with it
- Superior: Present the flip as "a lens worth considering", not "the truth"
- Beginner: Don't flip — translate the new definition into concrete changed behaviors

**Cognition Update (filling a gap):**
- MUST USE: Cross-Domain Synthesis (T5), Preview + Promise (H2)
- Structure: Pain, Concept, Process (T3)
- Learner: Concept section does heavy lifting — explain the new input thoroughly
- Peer: Concept section can be brief — they need the data point, not the explanation
- Superior: Lead with the data/insight, skip the pain section entirely, be dense
- Beginner: Break the new information into digestible pieces, connect each to their existing tasks

**Cognition Deepening (making it hit harder):**
- MUST USE: Evolution Ladder (H5), Gentle Confrontation ending (H6)
- Structure: Pain, Concept, Process (T3) OR Cross-Domain Synthesis (T5)
- Learner: "You've heard this, but here's why it hasn't clicked yet" — add the missing layer
- Peer: Add a new dimension they haven't considered (different domain, historical pattern)
- Superior: Provide the concrete evidence/data that makes an abstract principle actionable
- Beginner: Repetition is not a bug, it's a feature — restate with new examples each time

**Cognition Construction (building from scratch):**
- MUST USE: Teach-your-past-self (T11), Progressive Key Points (H4)
- Structure: Pain & Process (T2) with Gamify elements (T4)
- Learner: Start from the problem they recognize, build toward the concept they don't know
- Peer: Rare — but if needed, flag what's new and let them absorb at their own speed
- Superior: Frame as "background context for the decision at hand", keep it surgical
- Beginner: Full construction mode — map first, then one step at a time

### Writing Execution

**1. Hook**
- Attack the enemy (T1): a wrong definition, approach, or assumption
- Superior: Skip the attack — open with the insight or finding directly
- Set the transformation scene: "If you [desire], then [concrete first step] over [timeframe]"
- Beginner: Replace transformation scene with clear preview of what they'll be able to do

**2. Preview + Promise (H2)**
- State what's coming: "Here are N ideas on [topic]" or "By the end of this, you'll understand [X, Y, Z]"
- Superior: Can be one sentence. Beginner: Should be detailed.

**3. Key Points (progressive, not parallel — H4)**
- Each section builds on the previous one
- Cycle through Idea Legos (T8) as needed: Pain → Quote → Explanation → Example → Metaphor → Stat → Reframe → Action step
- Peer: Higher ratio of evidence-to-explanation. Beginner: Higher ratio of example-to-concept.

**4. Pre-emptive Objection Handling (H3)**
- Insert at the moment the reader would push back
- Use quoted dialogue: "But what about X?" — then address it
- Superior: Frame as "a reasonable concern is..." — never casual dialogue with superiors

**5. Ending**
- Gentle Confrontation (H6): hand responsibility back to the reader
- Superior: End with implications or recommended next steps, not confrontation
- Beginner: End with the single most important next action, nothing more

## Phase 4: Reception Path Audit (RUN AFTER EVERY DRAFT)

The writer's thinking path and the reader's reception path are almost never the same.

**After drafting, ALWAYS run this audit before presenting to the user:**

**Step 1: Identify the single takeaway.** What is the ONE thing the reader must walk away with? If you can't state it in one sentence, the piece is trying to do too much. Flag this to the user.

**Step 2: Trace the minimum path.** Working BACKWARD from the takeaway: what is the minimum set of ideas the reader needs to reach this conclusion themselves? If you remove an idea and the takeaway still lands, cut it.

**Step 3: Check for writer's-path contamination.** Scan every paragraph — is this here because the READER needs it, or because the WRITER thought it was interesting?
- **Throat-clearing**: First 1-3 paragraphs that "warm up" to the real point. The real point should BE the opening.
- **Tangent branches**: Interesting idea that doesn't serve the takeaway. Save for another piece.
- **Over-proving**: Same point 3 times with different evidence. Once convinced, move on.
- **Process narration**: "First I thought X, then realized Y" — reader needs your destination, not your journey. (Exception: when the journey IS the teaching.)
- **Defensive scaffolding**: Caveats and disclaimers protecting the writer, not helping the reader.

**Step 4: Length calibration by context and audience.**

**Work context** — the reader didn't choose to read this. You're borrowing their time.
- Learner: Medium length OK, but front-load the takeaway. Let depth be opt-in (they can stop after the key insight and still walk away with value).
- Peer: Short. Cut everything they already know. Get to the novel insight fast.
- Superior: As short as physically possible. If it can be 3 sentences, don't make it 3 paragraphs. Core message in the first line.
- Beginner: Structured and clear over short. Use headers, steps, numbered items. Length is OK, walls of text are not.

**Newsletter/public context** — the reader opted in. Earn their continued attention.
- Learner: Can be long — every paragraph must teach something. Length = value if density holds.
- Peer: Medium. Surprise them fast, then let the depth reward them for staying.
- Superior: Rarely your audience in this context. If so, treat like Peer.
- Beginner: Can be long but must feel effortless. Break into small, progressive sections. Celebrate the learning journey.

**Step 5: The "4pm Friday" test.** Imagine the reader gets this tired and distracted with 15 unread messages. Would they still get the takeaway? If not, restructure so the core hits within the first 3 sentences and everything else is supporting depth they can choose to read.

**Present audit results to the user.** If issues found, propose specific cuts or restructures.

## Key Principles

1. **Definition Flip > Argument**: Redefining a word is more powerful than arguing against it.
2. **Progressive > Parallel**: Key points should be a staircase, not a shelf.
3. **Educate into persuasion**: Teach the reader enough that they arrive at your position themselves.
4. **Signal > Volume**: One insight only you could produce beats ten generic points.
5. **Constraints > Freedom**: Frameworks liberate creativity, not restrict it.
6. **Borrow familiar structures, reassign meaning**: Use models people know, change what the axes mean.

# TECHNIQUE CATALOG

## Taught Techniques

**T1: Attack the Enemy (Hook)**
Open by identifying something the reader believes, does, or accepts that is hurting them. The "enemy": a past version of the reader, a common perspective, or a default behavior. Two paths: personal story or direct statement. Then set the transformation scene with "If you [desire]..." + benefit + first step + optional timeframe.

**T2: Pain & Process (Beginner Framework)**
Pain point → Step-by-step process. Steps can be reasons, tips, insights, or lessons. Use when the reader needs a clear actionable path.

**T3: Pain, Concept, Process (Intermediate Framework)**
Pain point → Deep concept from another domain → Step-by-step process. Usually start with the concept first, then build pain and process around it. The concept is the novel layer.

**T4: Perspective, Advantage, Gamify (Advanced Framework)**
How most people view the problem → How YOU view it → Steps designed like game levels (tiny, concrete, zero friction). Applies flow psychology. Use when changing behavior, not just informing.

**T5: Cross-Domain Synthesis**
Import a concept from a completely different field. The import must genuinely illuminate, not just decorate. The reader should think "I never would have connected these two things."

**T6: Micro Story (PAS)**
Problem → Amplify → Solution. Works at micro (paragraph) and macro (entire piece) levels. Best for short-form and section openers.

**T7: Pyramid Principle**
Conclusion first → 3-5 supporting arguments → Evidence. Answer-first. Ideal for peers and superiors. Memos, briefings, podcast answers.

**T8: Idea Legos**
Modular blocks to cycle through when stuck: pain point, quote, explanation, example, metaphor, statistic, goal statement ("If you want..."), action steps, personal story, reframe, what/how/why question.

**T9: Inner Album of Greatest Hits**
Your 8-10 core ideas refined through repetition. They connect to almost any topic. Repetition is refinement, not laziness.

**T10: Content Map**
Mission (anti-vision → vision) → 2-3 broad topics → 3-5 subtopics → 3-5 specific ideas per subtopic → Draw connections. Your niche is your mission, not a topic.

**T11: Teach Your Past Self**
Write as if explaining to yourself before you understood this. Assume 95% are beginners. Use 12-year-old language for complex concepts. "Learning something new" is the strongest retention mechanism.

## Hidden Techniques

**H1: Definition Flip**
Take a word your audience already has a mental model for and reassign its meaning. You're not arguing against their position — you're changing the language they think in. Once the language changes, conclusions change automatically.
Execute: (1) Identify the key word, (2) State/imply the common definition, (3) Redefine with your framing, (4) Build everything on the new definition.

**H2: Preview + Promise**
After the hook, tell the reader exactly what's coming. Pattern: "I want to share [N] ideas on [topic]." Or: "By the end of this, you'll understand [X, Y, Z]." Always use a specific number. Reduces cognitive load, builds commitment, filters readers.

**H3: Pre-emptive Objection via Fake Dialogue**
Before the reader formulates a pushback, voice it for them in quoted conversational format, then address it. Makes the reader feel heard before they've spoken. Use their actual language, not a straw man.

**H4: Progressive Key Points (Staircase, Not Shelf)**
Each section builds on the previous. Reader cannot understand section 3 without section 2. Creates narrative gravity — forces linear engagement.

**H5: Evolution Ladder**
Present a developmental hierarchy from low to high. Locate the reader. Show what's above. Two effects: self-location (clarity) and aspiration (motivation). Reader must be able to point to themselves — if they can't, the ladder is too abstract.

**H6: Gentle Confrontation (Ending)**
End by handing responsibility back. "Have you done all of this? Consistently?" / "I'll let you decide." Respects agency. More motivating than directive CTA. Creates lingering accountability.

**H7: Self-Referencing Web**
Link to past work at the moment of maximum reader curiosity on a sub-topic. Creates rabbit hole effect. Builds interconnected body of work.

**H8: Borrowed Authority via Recontextualization**
Use a well-known quote or thinker in an unexpected context. You're recontextualizing to serve YOUR argument. The original thinker might not have intended your interpretation — that's the point.

## Audience-Specific Technique Defaults

**Learner:** Lead with T1 + T11. Default structure T3. H1 is strongest tool (explain flip slowly). H5 helps self-locate.

**Peer:** Lead with H1 or T5 — surprise them. Default T7. H4 keeps engaged. H8 earns respect.

**Superior:** Lead with insight directly. Default T7. Use H2 for framing. NEVER use T1 or H6. End with next steps.

**Beginner:** Lead with T10 thinking (big picture first). Default T2 + T4. H5 for learning journey. T11 mandatory. Repeat with new examples.

# DEFINITION FLIP EXAMPLES

23 examples. The pattern: take a word people ALREADY have a mental model for, reassign what it means. Power is in hijacking existing neural pathways.

**1. T-shaped talent** — Common: horizontal=breadth, vertical=depth in specialty. Flip: horizontal="knowing" (facts), vertical="understanding" (cognitive OS sophistication). Effect: "depth" stops meaning "more about one thing" and starts meaning "higher-level thinking about everything."

**2. Genius thinking** — Common: high IQ, knowing a lot. Flip: "the ability to continue thinking" — not stopping too early. Effect: reframes intelligence as practice, not trait.

**3. Knowing vs Understanding** — Common: interchangeable. Flip: knowing=horizontal (facts), understanding=vertical (OS sophistication). Effect: "You can understand a lot while knowing very little."

**4. Work-life balance** — Common: equal time allocation. Flip: balance="the mediocre middle." Replacement: "contrast" — oscillating intensity. Effect: aspiration becomes avoidance.

**5. Work** — Common: drudgery for money. Flip: "vessel for sharing the best parts of yourself." Effect: desire to escape = misalignment symptom, not universal truth.

**6. Buddhist Middle Way** — Common: moderation. Flip: "dance between extremes." Effect: Buddhism-for-mediocrity argument collapses.

**7. Value-based content** — Common: educational how-to posts. Flip: that's commodity info. Real value = "content that changes behavior."

**8. Slop** — Common: binary label. Flip: spectrum from noise to art. Human slop existed before AI.

**9. Content creator** — Common: person who makes content. Flip: "content director" — value in vision and taste, not typing.

**10. Niche** — Common: specific topic. Flip: niche = your MISSION (the transformation). Frees multi-interest people.

**11. Meaning crisis** — Common: society problem. Flip: meaning as "scarce good" — scarcity creates value. Crisis IS opportunity.

**12. The Swap Test** — "If you could swap the creator and the creation would be just as valuable, AI can replace it." Personal diagnostic tool.

**13. Skills** — Common: specific capabilities. Flip: skills are "abstracting upward" — execution < judgment.

**14. Agency** — Common: taking action. Flip: "tendency to ITERATE without permission." Separates agency from impulsiveness.

**15. Conformity** — Common: peer pressure. Flip: "mind still connected by umbilical cord to society" — developmental stage, not behavior.

**16. Advertising** — Common: manipulation. Flip: natural act you already do unconsciously (recommending restaurants, dressing for dates).

**17. Starving artist** — Common: artists shouldn't care about money. Flip: "Historical artists were great marketers — that's why you know their name."

**18. Specialization** — Common: path to expertise. Flip: Adam Smith himself called specialized workers "as stupid and ignorant as possible." Authority FOR it becomes authority AGAINST it.

**19. Self-interest** — Common: selfish. Flip: "concern with one's own interest" — the only alternative is serving organizations that serve themselves.

**20. Shiny object syndrome** — Common: weakness, lack of focus. Flip: your curiosity "has been trying to tell you" specialization is the problem. The "syndrome" is healthy.

**21. Writing** — Common: putting words on page. Flip: "writing is thinking." Blank page = you don't have an idea yet.

**22. Writer's block** — Common: mysterious ailment. Flip: "you don't have an idea that fits the puzzle." Missing input, not a block.

**23. Newsletter** — Common: email to subscribers. Flip: "lead magnet AND stealth sales page" — simultaneously grows list and sells product.

## How to Create Your Own Definition Flips

1. Identify the key word your audience has a fixed meaning for
2. Ask: is their current definition part of the problem? If yes, flip opportunity
3. Find the reframe — what does this word ACTUALLY mean from your angle?
4. Test: does your entire argument become self-evident once they accept the new definition?
5. Deliver early — in the hook or first key point, everything after builds on it
6. Don't announce it — never say "let me redefine this." Just use the word differently
